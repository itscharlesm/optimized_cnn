Blues

The American Deep South is where the blues, as a musical genre and form, first emerged in the 1860s. Spirituals, labor songs, field hollers, chants, yells, and straightforward narrative ballads with rhymes from African-American culture were all absorbed into the blues.

Disco

Disco is a dance music genre and a subculture that originated in the metropolitan nightlife of the United States in the 1970s. Electric piano, synthesizers, electric rhythm guitars, string sections, brass and trumpets, and syncopated basslines are some of the elements that define its sound.

Hip-hop, sometimes referred to as rap, and formerly called disco rap, is a popular music genre that was developed in the Bronx, a borough of New York City, in the early 1970s by African Americans and Caribbean immigrants.

Jazz

Blues and ragtime are the musical genres' roots, and jazz emerged in the African-American neighborhoods of New Orleans, Louisiana, in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. It has been acknowledged as a significant medium for musical expression in both traditional and popular music since the Jazz Age in the 1920s. Swing and blue notes, intricate chords, call-and-response singers, polyrhythms, and improvisation are characteristics of jazz music. African rhythmic customs and European harmony are the sources of jazz.

Metal

Rock music known as "heavy metal," or simply "metal," originated in the United States and the United Kingdom throughout the late 1960s and early 1970s. Heavy metal bands originated from blues, psychedelic, and acid rock and created a massive, thick sound with distorted guitars, long guitar solos, strong beats, and volume.

Pop

Pop music is a subgenre of popular music that began to emerge in the United States and the United Kingdom in the middle of the 1950s. Pop music in the 1950s and 60s included rock & roll as well as the youth-oriented genres it influenced. Up until the late 1960s, pop and rock music were essentially indistinguishable. After that, pop started to be associated with more commercial, fleeting, and approachable music.

Reggae

Jamaica gave birth to the music genre known as reggae in the late 1960s. The phrase also refers to Jamaica's contemporary popular music and that of its diaspora. The first well-known song to utilize the term reggae was "Do the Reggay," a 1968 record by Toots and the Maytals that effectively gave the genre its name and exposed it to a worldwide audience.
